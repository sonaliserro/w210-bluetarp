Conclusion


Findings and Policy Recommendations

This research found that Puerto Rico did not receive an entirely equitable allocation of housing assistance aid, when compared to Florida and Texas. The findings indicate that aid was allocated consistently for housing repairs and replacement homes, which make up the majority of payments. The $49M Rental Assistance gap however indicates that the program’s policies restricted Puerto Rican’s from accessing rental assistance, in part due to a lack of alternative housing options in the aftermath of Hurricane Maria. To ensure the impacted communities are ultimately the recipients of funds that would otherwise be available to them, if were not due to a lack of alternative housing, a policy recommendation is proposed. FEMA could proactively assess the amount of alternate housing likely to be available following a hurricane and estimate whether there is likely to be a surplus in rental assistance funds. This money could then be reallocated to support an expedited recovery process for those most in need, such as communities living below the poverty line.

Limitations and Future Research

There are several limitations in terms of the scope of this study which present a path for future research. The first is that equitability is being assessed in terms of the financial amounts awarded to inspected applicants. The monetary award process is the final step in the aid allocation process and so future research may wish to focus on the type of applications that are selected for inspection, as obtaining a FEMA inspection is the gateway to securing financial aid.

Similarly, in order to truly assess the equitability of aid allocations it is necessary to look beyond the financial award process for inspected applicants. Subsequent research may wish to explore whether there are certain groups of people who did not apply, or were unable to apply, for Housing Assistance in the first place. Additionally, future research could leverage the methodologies applied in this study to evaluate other FEMA programs more broadly such as Other Needs Assistance and the larger Public Assistance Program.

Finally, it is recognized that this research is Puerto Rico centric. The recommendation proposed therefore may not generalize to mainland U.S. states, which differ in terms of socio-economic status and demographics. These are however, the very factors which make Puerto Rico the ideal candidate to evaluate whether FEMA’s policies serve all participating states and territories equally.
